Routine cyber hygiene also emphasizes using strong passwords rather than generic defaults to protect devices from attack attempts.

Recommended actions include:

*   Enable two-factor or multifactor authentication where possible.

These measures help manage access to systems effectively while reducing the risk associated with credential compromise (KU-AUTH-CREDENTIAL-001). Routine habits like checking computers for cyber threats and conducting security scans further complement these protective behaviors against potential attack attempts (KU-ROUTINE-CYBER-HYGIENE-PRACTICES-001).

Q: Does transmission hygiene include VPN use?
Context records: 3
A: According to Vishwanath et al.'s Cyber Hygiene Inventory, transmission hygiene does include VPN use alongside other browsing and connection safety practices beyond checking SSL indicators. While traditional definitions focus on verifying secure website connections via browser lock icons for unknown networks, this inventory expands the scope significantly. Transmission hygiene encompasses using a VPN when connecting via free or public Wi-Fi to mitigate risks associated with insecure wireless networks.

Key components of transmission hygiene identified in these sources include:
*   Using a VPN on free or public Wi-Fi.
*   Storing logins and passwords in encrypted online password vaults.
*   Placing online alerts for your name or personal information.

It is important to note that while these practices are listed as source-listed cyber hygiene behaviors, they do not guarantee complete safety against all threats. For instance, VPN use does not make public Wi-Fi completely safe, and password vaults eliminate no credential risk entirely. Users should remain cautious when connecting to unknown or insecure wireless networks rather than relying solely on SSL indicators for security assurance.
